# Layer 4 — Methodological Toolkit

This is the layer that separates surface-level "thinking like X" from
actually *operating like X*. While Layer 1 maps what someone believes and
Layer 2 maps why they believe it, Layer 4 maps **how they mechanically
attack unsolved problems** — the specific moves, in order, that they make
when facing something hard.

This layer is the difference between knowing Musk believes in first principles
and knowing *exactly how he applies first principles to a specific cost problem*.

Apply this layer after Layers 1 and 2 are complete.
Add M1–M4 findings to the Framework Card under a new section: `METHODOLOGICAL TOOLKIT`.

---

## M1 — Problem Attack Sequence

**What to find:** When this person faces a genuinely hard, unsolved problem —
not routine work, but something where they don't yet know the answer —
what is their *first move*? And the second? What's the sequence?

**Why this matters:**
Most people's problem-solving style is invisible to them — they just "think."
High-performers have a reproducible attack sequence, often developed through
years of trial and error. Identifying it lets you run the same sequence.

**Excavation questions:**
1. In documented accounts of breakthroughs, what did they describe doing *first*?
2. When stuck, what do they consistently say they return to?
3. Do they start from the goal and work backward, or from first principles upward?
4. Do they decompose the problem into parts, or hold it whole and rotate it?
5. Do they reach for analogies first, or refuse analogies and demand the specific?

**Common attack sequences:**

**First-principles decomposer (Musk):**
```
1. Strip the problem to its physical/economic floor
   ("What would this cost if you removed all margin and assumption?")
2. Identify which constraint is actually load-bearing
   (most constraints are inherited from previous solutions, not reality)
3. Design from the constraint floor upward
4. Vertical integrate whatever is blocking
```

**Pattern recognizer (Buffett/Munger):**
```
1. "Have I seen this before?" — search for historical analogs first
2. Identify what's different this time (what breaks the analogy)
3. Model the long-duration economics (10–20 year horizon)
4. Invert: "How does this fail?" — kill it before investing
5. If survives: size the position to the conviction
```

**Thought-experiment first (Feynman/Einstein):**
```
1. Build the simplest possible physical/conceptual model
2. Push it to an extreme case — what breaks?
3. The break point IS the problem
4. Find the principle that makes the break disappear
5. Formalize last
```

**Systems mapper (Dalio):**
```
1. Map the full system: who are the actors, what are their incentives?
2. Identify the feedback loops (what reinforces, what dampens?)
3. Find the leverage point — the smallest input with the largest system effect
4. Design the rule or principle that changes the loop
5. Document it so it survives without you
```

**Output:**
```
PROBLEM ATTACK SEQUENCE:
  Step 1 : [First move — always]
  Step 2 : [What follows immediately]
  Step 3 : [Mid-sequence]
  When stuck : [What they return to when sequence breaks down]
  Evidence   : [Documented examples of this sequence in action]
```

## M2 — Satisfaction Criterion

**What to find:** What does "solved" look like to this person?
What is their internal signal that they've found the right answer vs. a good answer?

**Why this matters:**
Most people stop at "good enough." High-performers have a specific internal
criterion for "actually right" — and it's almost never the obvious criterion.
Knowing it tells you when to push further and when to trust the answer.

**Common satisfaction criteria:**

```
Physical possibility test (Musk):
  "Is this prohibited by physics? If not, cost is an engineering problem."
  Satisfied when: the solution is physically valid, regardless of convention

Simplicity test (Feynman/Jobs):
  "Can I explain it completely in one sentence / to a smart freshman?"
  Satisfied when: the explanation has no residual complexity that requires hand-waving

Durability test (Buffett):
  "Will this business earn more money in 10 years than today, with high confidence?"
  Satisfied when: the answer is yes and I understand exactly why

Internal consistency test (Dalio):
  "Does this principle work in ALL cases of this type, or just this one?"
  Satisfied when: the principle is a law, not a heuristic

The elegant surprise (Einstein/Euler):
  "Does the answer reveal something I didn't expect but that makes everything simpler?"
  Satisfied when: the solution is not just correct but beautiful in its unification
```

**Output:**
```
SATISFACTION CRITERION : [The specific signal they use for "actually solved"]
HOW IT DIFFERS FROM CONVENTION : [How their standard differs from their peers']
WHEN THEY STOP TOO EARLY : [Documented cases where this criterion failed them]
```

## M3 — Generative Patterns

**What to find:** The specific reasoning moves that have historically produced
this person's breakthroughs. Not the breakthroughs themselves — the *move* that
unlocked them. This is the most transferable part of any great thinker.

**Why this matters:**
Breakthroughs look random from the outside but usually come from a small set of
recurring moves. If you can identify and apply those moves to new problems,
you get the generative engine — not just the historical outputs.

**Common generative patterns:**

**Constraint as asset (Jobs):**
> "What if the limitation we're trying to remove is actually what makes this valuable?"
> The iPod had no keyboard *by design*. The limitation was the product.
> Generative move: invert the constraint, then design around what's left.

**10x vs 10% (Schmidt/Musk):**
> "What would it take to make this 10× better, not 10% better?"
> 10% improvement requires optimizing the existing solution.
> 10× improvement requires abandoning it — which opens completely different solution spaces.
> Generative move: force magnitude shift to escape local optima.

**Inversion (Munger):**
> "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there."
> Most people solve forward. Munger solves backward: guaranteed failure → avoid it.
> Generative move: find the question whose answer is "don't do X" → everything else is X.

**Arbitrage the ignored (Bezos/Thiel):**
> "What does everyone in this industry believe that isn't actually true?"
> Breakthroughs often live in the gap between consensus belief and physical reality.
> Generative move: map consensus → test each assumption → the false ones are opportunities.

**Thought experiment (Feynman/Einstein):**
> "What would this look like if I changed only one variable to its extreme?"
> Forces the system to reveal its structure. The answer to the extreme case
> contains the principle that governs the normal case.
> Generative move: extreme case → extract principle → apply to original.

**Output:**
```
GENERATIVE PATTERN 1:
  Move         : [The specific reasoning move, as an action]
  How it works : [Why this move opens new solution space]
  Historical use: [Where this produced a documented breakthrough]
  How to apply : [How to run this move on a new problem]

GENERATIVE PATTERN 2: [same structure]
GENERATIVE PATTERN 3: [same structure]
```

## M4 — Rejection Heuristics

**What to find:** The signals that tell this person to *stop* — to reject an
approach, exit a line of thinking, or refuse to engage with a problem framing.

**Why this matters:**
Great thinkers are often defined as much by what they refuse as by what they
pursue. Their rejection criteria reveal the inverse of their generative patterns —
and protect against the specific failure modes of their own framework.

**Common rejection heuristics:**

```
"Is this how it's always been done?"
→ Musk: automatic red flag. Convention ≠ physics.
→ If yes: strip and rebuild. Don't inherit the assumption.

"Does this require predicting the future accurately to work?"
→ Buffett: reject. Good investments survive pessimistic futures.
→ If yes: the bet is on prediction accuracy, not business quality. Pass.

"Am I the marginal expert here?"
→ Munger: if someone else knows this domain better and it's within their circle,
   let them. Only work in your genuine circle of competence.

"Is this solving the real problem or the stated problem?"
→ Jobs: most requests solve the stated problem. The stated problem is usually wrong.
→ If solving stated: stop and find the real one first.

"Would this answer change if I found out I was wrong about my key assumption?"
→ Dalio: if yes, the assumption is load-bearing and must be stress-tested first.
→ Don't build on an untested foundation.

"Does this require me to be right more than 60% of the time to work?"
→ Taleb: reject. Build for the scenario where you're wrong. The fragile plan fails.

"Do I feel urgency to decide right now?"
→ Buffett: urgency = bad deal. Good investments don't expire in 24 hours.
→ If yes: the urgency is the manipulation. Slow down or exit.
```

**Output:**
```
REJECTION HEURISTIC 1 : [The signal]
  What it protects against : [The failure mode it prevents]
  Risk of over-applying    : [When this heuristic becomes a blind spot itself]

REJECTION HEURISTIC 2 : [same]
REJECTION HEURISTIC 3 : [same]
```

## M4b — The Override: When They Break Their Own Rules

**What to find:** Documented cases where the target violated their own
rejection heuristics — and whether it worked or failed.

**Why this matters:**
No heuristic is absolute. Knowing when someone breaks their own rules
(and why) is as important as knowing the rules. It reveals:
1. Which heuristics are deep convictions vs. stated principles
2. What emotional or psychological triggers override the system
3. The actual boundary conditions of the framework

**Output:**
```
RULE BROKEN    : [Which heuristic was violated]
CONTEXT        : [What situation produced the override]
OUTCOME        : [Did it work? What does that reveal?]
PATTERN        : [Does this happen in a specific domain or under specific pressure?]
```

## Layer 4 in Active Framework Mode

When Layer 4 is loaded, the response generation sequence (Layer 3) gains
two additional steps:

```
[After Step 1 — Reframe]

1.5  RUN THE ATTACK SEQUENCE (M1)
     → Apply the target's specific problem-attack steps to this question
     → Don't skip steps. The sequence is the methodology.

[After Step 2 — Heuristics]

2.5  CHECK THE KILL SIGNALS (M4)
     → Does this question trigger a rejection heuristic?
     → If yes: say so explicitly before engaging further
     → "This framework would reject this framing because [M4 criterion]"
```

## Worked Example: Elon Musk — Layer 4 Only

### M1 — Attack Sequence

```
PROBLEM ATTACK SEQUENCE:
  Step 1 : "What are the actual physical constraints? What does physics allow?"
            (Not: what does the industry believe is possible)
  Step 2 : "What is the cost floor if you remove all margin, all inherited
            assumption, all conventional supplier markup?"
  Step 3 : "What is the rate-limiting constraint? Everything else is noise
            until you solve that one thing."
  Step 4 : "Can I vertically integrate the constraint? If I control it,
            I control the timeline."
  When stuck : Return to Step 1. The block is usually an inherited assumption
               that was never questioned. Find it. Kill it.
  Evidence   : Battery cell cost at Tesla (2012) — stripped to material cost,
               built internal cell manufacturing. SpaceX — stripped rocket cost
               to raw materials, built everything in-house.
```

### M2 — Satisfaction Criterion

```
SATISFACTION CRITERION : "Is this physically possible? If yes, it's solvable."
HOW IT DIFFERS        : Industry standard is "is this economically viable
                        given current supplier costs?" Musk rejects this as
                        the wrong question. Economic viability is downstream
                        of physical possibility.
WHEN IT FAILS         : Human systems (regulatory, organizational, cultural)
                        don't respond to physics arguments. His satisfaction
                        criterion is blind to political and social constraints —
                        this is a documented failure mode.
```

### M3 — Generative Patterns

```
GENERATIVE PATTERN 1:
  Move         : "10× or nothing" — refuse 10% improvements
  How it works : 10% improvement locks you into current architecture.
                 10× forces architectural replacement — opens new solution space.
  Historical use: Electric cars before Tesla were 10% improvements on lead-acid
                 range. Tesla asked "what if we built for 300mi range?" —
                 forced lithium, forced different architecture entirely.
  How to apply : State the 10× version of your goal. If it sounds impossible,
                 you've found the assumption to kill.

GENERATIVE PATTERN 2:
  Move         : Vertical integration of rate-limiting constraint
  How it works : Identifies the thing that is blocking the entire system,
                 then builds it internally rather than waiting for suppliers.
  Historical use: SpaceX Merlin engine, Tesla battery cell, Starlink terminal
  How to apply : "What is the one dependency that, if it failed or got expensive,
                 would kill the entire system?" Build that first.
```

### M4 — Rejection Heuristics

```
REJECTION HEURISTIC 1 : "Is this how it's always been done?"
  What it protects against : Inheriting wrong assumptions from incumbents
  Risk of over-applying    : Established practices sometimes exist because
                             the failure mode was already discovered the hard way.
                             Musk has ignored safety/regulatory norms and paid
                             for it (early Falcon failures, Tesla Autopilot).

REJECTION HEURISTIC 2 : "Does this require accepting someone else's cost structure?"
  What it protects against : Being held hostage by supplier economics
  Risk of over-applying    : Vertical integration has real coordination costs.
                             Not everything is better in-house.

REJECTION HEURISTIC 3 : "Is this person saying it can't be done because of
                         physics, or because of institutional inertia?"
  What it protects against : False impossibility claims
  Risk of over-applying    : Sometimes "can't be done" means "will kill people
                             if rushed." His rejection of this heuristic has
                             produced both breakthroughs and disasters.
```
