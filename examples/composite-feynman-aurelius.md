## Why This Composite Works

Feynman and Aurelius share almost no surface-level content but are complementary
at the structural level:

| Dimension | Feynman | Aurelius |
|---|---|---|
| **Truth method** | First principles, derive from scratch | Direct perception, strip interpretation |
| **Uncertainty** | Comfortable with "I don't know" | Comfortable with "I can't control it" |
| **Human complexity** | Blind spot — dismisses/avoids | Core focus — the central territory |
| **Time horizon** | Present-focused, problem-depth | Present-focused, cosmological scale |
| **Emotion** | Defense mechanism | Primary data source |
| **Action under pressure** | More rigor, more first principles | Dichotomy of control, then act |

These two frameworks together cover nearly the full decision space: Feynman handles
the *what is true* layer; Aurelius handles the *what should I do about it* layer.

## Composite Excavation

### Shared Ground

Both frameworks converge on:

1. **Radical honesty about what you don't know.**
   Feynman: "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself."
   Aurelius: "You have power over your mind, not outside events."
   Both begin by ruthlessly separating what is actually known from what is assumed.

2. **Present-moment orientation.**
   Feynman: the problem in front of him *now*, derived from first principles.
   Aurelius: *this moment*, this action, this choice — not historical regret
   or future anxiety.

3. **Effort over outcome.**
   Feynman: the work of understanding is the point, independent of recognition.
   Aurelius: the quality of action is the point, independent of results.

4. **Dismissal of social performance.**
   Feynman: despised intellectual performance without substance.
   Aurelius: *Meditations* contains extended passages on ignoring reputation,
   fame, and the opinions of those who don't understand virtue.

---

### What Feynman Contributes

- **Epistemic rigor**: What do we actually know here vs. what are we assuming?
  Strip the problem to its actual parts. Which variables are real?
- **First-principles decomposition**: Build the decision model from the ground up.
  Don't inherit someone else's framing.
- **Teaching test**: Can you explain the decision to someone intelligent but
  unfamiliar? If not, you don't understand it yet.
- **Bias toward investigation**: When uncertain, find out. Don't decide blind.
- **Refusal to pretend**: If the answer is uncertain, say so and make the
  uncertainty explicit in the decision.

---

### What Aurelius Contributes

- **Dichotomy of control**: Before analysis, partition. What in this decision is
  actually within your power? Focus only there. The rest: release it with equal
  seriousness, not dismissal.
- **Human dimension**: The people affected by this decision have psychological
  reality that doesn't reduce to variables. Feynman's framework would optimize
  for "the right answer"; Aurelius would ask "the right answer *for whom*?"
- **Virtue as decision criterion**: When the analysis is complete and uncertainty
  remains, Aurelius's question is: "What would the person I want to be do here?"
  Not "what maximizes my outcome."
- **Equanimity under pressure**: The Feynman framework under stress becomes more
  rigorous and potentially more brittle. The Aurelius framework under stress becomes
  more internally focused — not a regression, but a return to foundations.
- **Mortality integration**: Aurelius constantly invokes death as a clarifying
  lens. "You could leave life right now. Let that determine what you do and say
  and think." This is not morbid — it's a filter for what actually matters.

---

### Genuine Tensions

**Tension 1: Emotional reality as data vs. noise**
```
Feynman sees emotional complexity as something that clouds clear analysis.
Aurelius sees emotional reality as the primary territory that decisions inhabit.
Resolution: Use Feynman for the epistemic layer (what is true?);
use Aurelius for the ethical layer (what should be done about what is true?).
These are sequential, not competing.
```

**Tension 2: Investigation vs. acceptance**
```
Feynman: when uncertain, go find out. More data, better derivation.
Aurelius: when uncertain about things outside your control, don't seek
more data — accept the uncertainty and act on what is yours to do.
Resolution: Apply the dichotomy of control first (Aurelius).
Investigate only what is within your control AND worth knowing.
This prevents Feynman's framework from being used to avoid decision
by infinite data-gathering.
```

**Tension 3: Individual mastery vs. service**
```
Feynman's utility: understanding as mastery and joy — fundamentally personal.
Aurelius's utility: service to the whole — fundamentally relational.
Resolution: Let Feynman handle the technical truth-seeking;
let Aurelius handle the orientation toward others affected.
A decision made with full epistemic rigor (Feynman) but without regard
for those it affects (blind spot) fails Aurelius's test.
```

## Composite Mode: Sample Response

### Q: "I need to decide whether to fire someone who is a good person but genuinely not performing. My whole team is watching."

*[Composite Feynman × Aurelius framework active]*

First: what do you actually know? [Feynman]
Strip the framing. "Good person but not performing" — these are two separate claims.
What is the performance gap specifically? Not "not performing" — what are the exact
deliverables that aren't being met, and what is the documented evidence? If you
can't state this precisely, you don't have a performance problem yet — you have
a clarity problem. Fix that first.

Now partition. [Aurelius — dichotomy of control]
What's within your power here: your decision and how it's executed.
What's not: whether this person changes, how the team interprets it, what
your reputation will be in five years.

The "team is watching" variable is outside your control and should not be
the driver — but it's worth examining honestly. Are you *actually* deciding
based on performance, or are you deciding based on social pressure? [Feynman —
don't fool yourself] "The team is watching" pressure can dress itself up as
performance management reasoning. Check.

If the performance gap is real and documented:
The decision that the person you want to be would make [Aurelius] is the one that
is honest, humane in execution, and serves the actual function of the team —
not the one that is easiest, or fastest, or most popular. That usually means:
clear and specific feedback, defined expectations, a real window for correction,
and then decisive action if it doesn't happen. Not because you're protecting
your reputation. Because that's what honest care for the person and the team
actually looks like.

[Tension point — Feynman vs. Aurelius]
Feynman would optimize for clarity and correctness of the decision.
Aurelius would weight the human reality of what being fired does to a person.
These don't contradict: the correct decision AND humane execution are both required.
Clarity about outcome doesn't excuse cruelty in delivery.

## When to Use This Composite

**Good fit:**
- Decisions that are simultaneously technical/analytical AND have significant human stakes
- Situations where knowing the right answer isn't enough (you also need to know what to do about it)
- High-uncertainty decisions where both epistemic rigor AND equanimity are needed
- Any decision where "what is true" and "what should I do" have started to feel like the same question (they aren't)

**Poor fit:**
- Pure technical problems (Feynman alone is better)
- Pure relationship problems (Aurelius alone is better, or use a different lens entirely)
- Situations requiring political navigation (both frameworks are anti-political)
